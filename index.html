How ESG Reporting Standards (IFRS S1 & S2, CSRD) Impact Indian Businesses in 2025

The convergence of global ESG reporting standards with India's evolving sustainability framework is creating significant compliance challenges and opportunities for Indian businesses. While India maintains its own Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) framework, the influence of international standards like IFRS S1 & S2 and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is reshaping corporate disclosure practices across sectors.

Current ESG Regulatory Landscape in India

India's ESG reporting framework is primarily governed by the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) through the BRSR mandate, which requires the top 1,000 listed companies by market capitalization to report on environmental, social, and governance metrics. This framework has been gradually expanding, with discussions around extending requirements to smaller listed entities and eventual adoption of international standards.

The Ministry of Corporate Affairs (MCA) has been actively engaging with global standard-setters to evaluate the integration of international frameworks with existing Indian regulations. This alignment process aims to reduce reporting burden while maintaining compliance with both domestic and international investor expectations.

IFRS S1 & S2: Global Sustainability Disclosure Standards

IFRS S1 (General Requirements for Disclosure of Sustainability-related Financial Information) establishes the foundation for sustainability disclosures, requiring companies to provide material information about sustainability-related risks and opportunities that could affect cash flows, financing access, or cost of capital over the short, medium, and long term.

IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) specifically addresses climate-related risks and opportunities, mandating detailed disclosures about governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics and targets related to climate change. This standard requires companies to disclose Scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas emissions, creating significant data collection and verification challenges.

Implementation Timeline and Indian Adoption

While IFRS S1 and S2 became effective globally from January 1, 2024, India has not yet mandated their adoption. However, many Indian multinational corporations with international operations or foreign investors are voluntarily adopting these standards to maintain global competitiveness and access to international capital markets.

The Institute of Chartered Accountants of India (ICAI) has been conducting capacity-building programs to prepare Indian companies and professionals for eventual adoption. Industry experts anticipate that formal adoption could occur within the next 2-3 years, likely through a phased approach starting with large listed companies.

CSRD: European Union's Comprehensive Framework

The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) represents one of the most comprehensive ESG reporting frameworks globally, requiring detailed disclosures across environmental, social, and governance dimensions. While primarily applicable to EU companies, the CSRD significantly impacts Indian businesses through several channels:

Direct Impact on Indian Companies

  • EU Subsidiaries: Indian companies with significant EU operations or subsidiaries must comply with CSRD requirements.
  • Supply Chain Requirements: Indian suppliers to EU companies face increased due diligence and sustainability reporting demands.
  • Financial Services: Indian companies seeking EU financing or listing on European exchanges must meet CSRD-aligned disclosure standards.

Value Chain Implications

The CSRD's emphasis on value chain sustainability creates ripple effects throughout global supply chains. Indian manufacturers, particularly in textiles, pharmaceuticals, automotive, and technology sectors, are experiencing increased pressure from EU customers to provide detailed sustainability data and demonstrate compliance with environmental and social standards.

Sector-Specific Impacts on Indian Businesses

Financial Services and Banking

Indian banks and financial institutions are facing heightened scrutiny regarding ESG integration in lending and investment decisions. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) has been developing guidelines for climate risk disclosures, partially aligned with international standards. Key impacts include:

  • Enhanced Due Diligence: Banks must assess and disclose climate-related risks in their loan portfolios.
  • Green Finance Reporting: Detailed tracking and reporting of sustainable finance products and their impact.
  • Stress Testing: Implementation of climate scenario analysis for portfolio risk assessment.

Manufacturing and Heavy Industries

India's manufacturing sector, particularly steel, cement, chemicals, and automotive industries, faces significant challenges in meeting international ESG disclosure requirements:

  • Carbon Intensity Reporting: Mandatory disclosure of Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions with verification requirements.
  • Water and Waste Management: Comprehensive reporting on resource consumption and waste generation.
  • Supply Chain Transparency: Enhanced due diligence and reporting on supplier sustainability practices.

Information Technology and Services

The Indian IT sector, with its global client base, is increasingly aligning with international ESG standards:

  • Digital Carbon Footprint: Reporting on energy consumption of data centers and digital infrastructure.
  • Diversity and Inclusion: Enhanced disclosure requirements on workforce diversity and inclusion metrics.
  • Cybersecurity and Data Privacy: Integration of governance-related disclosures with sustainability reporting.

Implementation Challenges for Indian Businesses

Data Collection and Management

  • Infrastructure Limitations: Many Indian companies lack sophisticated data collection systems for ESG metrics.
  • Third-Party Verification: Limited availability of qualified ESG auditors and verification services in India.
  • Supply Chain Data: Difficulty in obtaining reliable sustainability data from suppliers, particularly smaller vendors.

Cost and Resource Implications

  • Technology Infrastructure: Investment in ESG data management platforms and reporting tools.
  • Human Resources: Training existing staff and hiring ESG specialists.
  • External Services: Engaging consultants, auditors, and rating agencies for compliance and verification.

Regulatory Alignment

  • Dual Compliance: Meeting both BRSR requirements and international standards.
  • Timing Misalignment: Different reporting cycles and deadlines across frameworks.
  • Standard Evolution: Keeping pace with evolving international standards while maintaining domestic compliance.

Strategic Opportunities and Business Benefits

Access to International Capital

Companies demonstrating strong ESG performance and comprehensive reporting are accessing preferential financing terms and broader investor bases. International institutional investors increasingly use ESG criteria for investment decisions, making robust reporting a competitive advantage.

Operational Efficiency

ESG reporting initiatives often identify operational inefficiencies and cost-saving opportunities:

  • Energy Management: Improved tracking leading to reduced energy consumption and costs.
  • Waste Reduction: Enhanced monitoring resulting in material cost savings and operational efficiency.
  • Risk Management: Better identification and mitigation of environmental and social risks.

Market Differentiation

Strong ESG performance and transparent reporting enhance brand value and market positioning, particularly important for Indian companies seeking to expand globally or attract conscious consumers.

Preparation Strategies for Indian Businesses

Immediate Actions

  • Gap Analysis: Conduct comprehensive assessment of current ESG reporting capabilities against international standards.
  • Data Systems: Invest in robust ESG data collection and management systems.
  • Stakeholder Engagement: Establish clear communication channels with investors, customers, and regulators regarding ESG strategy.

Medium-term Planning

  • Standard Alignment: Gradually align existing BRSR reporting with IFRS S1 & S2 requirements.
  • Supply Chain Integration: Develop supplier sustainability programs and data collection mechanisms.
  • Capacity Building: Invest in training and development of ESG expertise within the organization.

Long-term Strategic Integration

  • Business Strategy Alignment: Integrate ESG considerations into core business strategy and decision-making processes.
  • Innovation Focus: Develop products and services that address sustainability challenges.
  • Stakeholder Value Creation: Use ESG performance as a driver of long-term stakeholder value creation.

The evolution of ESG reporting standards represents both a significant compliance challenge and a strategic opportunity for Indian businesses. Companies that proactively prepare for international standard adoption while maintaining excellence in domestic reporting requirements will be best positioned to thrive in the evolving global business environment. Success will require substantial investment in data infrastructure, human capital development, and strategic integration of sustainability considerations into core business operations.
